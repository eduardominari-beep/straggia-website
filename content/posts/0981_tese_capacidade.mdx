### Core idea

I used to hear that strategy fails because teams cannot execute. Heavy culture, disengaged people, unprepared leadership, distant top management, lack of accountability‚Ä¶ the diagnostics usually sound reasonable.

Today I think differently. I believe we can make mistakes everywhere:

* Translating the thesis into goals
* Choosing the few projects that really move the needle
* Building management routines that sustain the change
* Installing operational capacity to match the ambition we designed

‚Ä¶and still walk away saying ‚Äúculture‚Äù killed execution.

Culture matters. But on its own, it does not solve the execution problem. In reality, culture is often a consequence of how the organization structures work, goals, agreements and the flow of information.

When we talk about strategic execution, we are, in practice, talking about installing capacity inside an organization.

And that leads us to the central idea of this piece: **strategy that does not become installed capability is just a bundle of good intentions**.

### Thesis vs. capability

In strategy, we like to talk about thesis.

The thesis is the structured narrative about how we will win: which markets we will play in, how we will differentiate, which levers we will pull, what risks we will take, what trade-offs we accept.

It is essential. Without a sound thesis, any move becomes a loose tactic.

But the thesis alone does not change reality. What changes reality is installed capability.

Capability is what the organization can do repeatedly, consistently, at reasonable scale. It is not a slide deck, a workshop or a set of motivational slogans.

Capability is:

* Being able to open X new stores per year at a minimum quality level.
* Shipping Y relevant features to production every quarter.
* Serving Z% of customers in up to N minutes with satisfaction above a certain threshold.
* Integrating acquisitions without destroying value or culture along the way.

Capability is what the company can already execute today, even if people rotate, even if the CEO travels, even if the founder goes on vacation.

If it does not turn into capability, everything depends on individual heroes, on saviour leaders or on ‚Äúninja‚Äù teams that carry the weight. That does not scale.

### The four conversations that get mixed up

When we talk about strategic execution, we usually mix four different conversations:

1. **About the thesis**: What game are we in? How will we win? Where are the value pools?
2. **About goals**: What do we want to see in terms of concrete outcomes? In what time frame? With which trade-offs?
3. **About projects**: What needs to change, be built or be eliminated so the thesis becomes real? What concrete moves will we make?
4. **About capability**: What must the organization be consistently able to do to sustain results over time?

In practice, what I see most often is:

* A lot of time spent on the thesis (slides, analysis, benchmarking, strong statements).
* Some time on goals (often poorly defined or too many of them).
* An explosion of projects (everyone wants a ‚Äústrategic project‚Äù in the portfolio).
* Very little focus on **capability**.

The result: an inflated portfolio, overloaded teams, many ‚Äústrategic‚Äù initiatives that never finish ‚Äî and very little structural change in how the company actually works.

### Installed capability is more than a well-run project

Projects matter. Without projects, we do not change anything meaningful.

But a well-run project does **not** guarantee installed capability.

A project has a beginning, middle and end. There is scope, schedule, budget. There is a dedicated team, sometimes consultants, a PMO and a lot of attention from top management.

Capability, on the other hand, is what keeps working once the project is over.

If, at the end of the project, you still depend on the same three heroic people to sustain the result, you have not installed capability. You have installed dependency.

Installed capability means:

* There is a clear process, with a defined owner and explicit responsibilities.
* There are indicators that show whether it is performing or not.
* The organization knows what to do when things go off track (routines for analysis and correction).
* People involved understand why that capability exists and what impact it has on the whole.

If the result depends only on extra effort, goodwill or constant improvisation, we are not talking about capability. We are talking about survival.

### From thesis to capability, feet on the ground

In practical terms, the path from thesis to capability climbs a set of very concrete steps:

1. **From the thesis to the few goals that matter**  
   A good thesis drives focus. Instead of twenty priorities, you choose three to five outcomes that truly define whether the strategy is working. It is not ‚Äúdoing many things‚Äù; it is **moving a few critical indicators**.

2. **From goals to a lean portfolio of transformational projects**  
   Each critical goal must be translated into a small set of high-impact projects. This is not a wish list; it is ‚Äúwhat we must change/build/eliminate to get there‚Äù. If it does not fit within the real capacity of the organization, it is self-deception.

3. **From projects to processes, routines and clear ownership**  
   When the project ends, someone has to own the day-to-day. Who is the owner? What is the process? Which indicator will we track? In what meeting does it show up? What happens when the result deteriorates?

4. **From ownership to management systems that sustain capability**  
   Here we talk about agendas, forums, cadences, rituals. In which recurring meetings does leadership look at these indicators? Where do we discuss root causes? Where are resources, trade-offs and adjustments decided? Without this, capability decays.

5. **From management systems to observable culture and behaviour**  
   When the system works, it starts shaping behaviour: people come to meetings knowing the numbers, discuss facts and causes, suggest improvements, align expectations. Culture gradually stops being a speech and becomes practice.

### Governance, management systems and routine

Three unglamorous words, but fundamental for capability:

* **Governance**: Who decides what? At which level? Using which criteria? How is alignment between strategy, operations and people maintained?
* **Management systems**: What are the recurring forums for decision-making, monitoring and learning? At what cadence? With what inputs?
* **Routine**: What happens every week, every month, every quarter, regardless of who is in charge at that moment?

Without this, the company lives by campaign. Each quarter has a fashionable theme. Each year, a new strategic slogan.

With this, the company builds a backbone of decisions, conversations and rituals that sustain installed capability.
